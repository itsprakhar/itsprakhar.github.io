I have been doing machine learning since 2018, before it became everyone's headline. I came up through full-stack web development and made a deliberate move into ML at a point when the field looked nothing like it does today. That head start shaped how I think: less impressed by leaderboards, more interested in what actually holds up when a system has to run in the real world.

Today I am a Senior Staff Engineer in computer vision, working on **ambient intelligence** for healthcare. The systems I build understand activity in physical environments from live camera feeds, in settings where lighting is difficult, occlusion is constant, privacy is non-negotiable, and a wrong answer has real cost. It has to work, not just benchmark well.

My current research is in **self-supervised and representation learning**, in particular temporal JEPA-style objectives that learn useful structure from unlabeled video. The goal is practical: reduce the labeling burden when deploying to new environments, and learn representations that genuinely transfer rather than memorize one room. Alongside that, I think a lot about the philosophy of mind as it bears on AI architecture, especially the question of whether forcing internal representations to be human-comprehensible could help with alignment and hallucination.

I hold an MSc in Machine Learning and AI from Liverpool John Moores University, a PG Diploma from IIIT Bangalore, and a TRIZ Level 1 certification in structured inventive problem solving, which I think is one of the most underrated thinking tools in engineering.
